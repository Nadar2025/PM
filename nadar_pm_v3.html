01 / PROBLEM & OPPORTUNITY

The queue was killing repeat business

Sahal's Cafe had strong loyalty but a broken ordering experience. During peak morning hours (7–9am), the counter queue backed up, verbal orders caused errors, and regulars had no mechanism to skip the wait. There was zero data on what customers ordered, no retention hook, and staff were context-switching constantly.

The business opportunity: convert habitual customers into app users, reduce order errors, and introduce a lightweight loyalty program to increase visit frequency — all without hiring more staff.

"I just want to tap twice and have my oat milk latte ready when I get there. That's it."

— Research participant · Daily customer
02 / DISCOVERY & PRIORITIZATION

5 interviews, 1 competitive audit, 3 core bets

I ran 5 user interviews with regular café customers and observed one full morning rush. Competitive audit of Starbucks, Blue Bottle, and Philz surfaced a key insight: every major café app is over-engineered for corporate scale. A community café needs radical simplicity, not feature parity.

04 / KEY DECISIONS & TRADEOFFS

What we cut, what we kept, and why

No account creation for first order. The original spec required sign-up before ordering. I cut this after 3 of 5 research participants said they'd abandon rather than create an account. We moved to guest checkout with optional account creation post-order.

Loyalty as ambient progress, not a tab. An early design had a dedicated "Rewards" nav item. I pushed back — users shouldn't need to seek out their progress. We surfaced it as a subtle progress bar on the order confirmation screen. Low engineering effort, high retention value.

Group ordering deferred to v2. High-complexity, affecting the cart data model and confirmation UX. With a 7-week timeline, I scoped it out to ship a clean solo experience first. Business validated this — solo orders are 85% of transactions.

05 / REFLECTION & NEXT BETS

What I'd prioritize in v2

Staff-side order queue (P0): The biggest gap is operational. Mobile orders arrive without a barista-facing display. A staff queue view would close the loop and justify scaling adoption. Highest-ROI v2 feature.

Retention analytics (P0): We shipped without instrumentation to measure app-driven repeat visit rate. I'd instrument this first — without data, loyalty program decisions are guesswork.

Accessibility audit: Tap targets on modifier toggles are undersized. P0 fix before any wider rollout.

01 / PROBLEM STATEMENT

A high-stakes decision with a low-trust website

Choosing childcare is one of the highest-stakes decisions a parent makes. SafeSpace Daycare had built a strong in-person reputation — but their website, a generic GoDaddy template, was actively undermining that trust. Parents reported feeling confused, underinformed, and unable to find basic information like daily schedules and enrollment steps.

Business problem: The website was producing friction at the top of the funnel. Prospective parents bounced before converting to a tour or inquiry.

"I couldn't figure out what age groups they take or what a typical day looks like. I just gave up and called someone else."

— Research synthesis · Parent interview
02 / DISCOVERY & REQUIREMENTS

What parents actually need before they trust you

I conducted parent interviews and a heuristic audit of the existing site, identifying a core information hierarchy failure: content was organized around what the daycare wanted to say, not what parents came to find.

Parent JTBDOriginal SiteRedesign Solution
Verify safety & credentialsBuried, no structureAbove-fold trust signals, staff bios
Understand daily scheduleNot presentVisual schedule on Programs page
Know age groups & pricingOne paragraph, unclearProgram cards with age tags + CTA
Contact or book a tourFooter email onlyPersistent CTA, contact modal
Get a sense of the vibeStock photos, cold feelWarm photography, rounded design
03 / STAKEHOLDER MANAGEMENT

Managing the owner's instincts vs. user data

The daycare owner wanted to lead with awards and history. Parents wanted practical info first. Classic stakeholder-vs-user tension. I ran a card sort with 4 parents and presented the results in a stakeholder review.

Parents ranked "Daily schedule" and "Age groups & pricing" as the top two homepage priorities — "About us / History" ranked last. The owner agreed to restructure after seeing the data. Using evidence to depersonalize disagreements is one of the most valuable PM skills.

04 / OUTCOMES & LEARNINGS

Trust is a product feature

Early feedback showed significant improvements in content clarity, navigation simplicity, and emotional tone. Parents described the redesigned site as calm, organized, and easy to understand — the exact outcomes defined at kickoff.

Key learning: In high-trust industries (childcare, healthcare, finance), the emotional quality of the digital experience is not a "nice-to-have" — it is the product.

What's next: Instrument the site with conversion tracking (tour booking rate, contact form submissions) to tie design decisions to business outcomes. Currently success is measured qualitatively — the quantitative loop needs to close.

01 / OVERVIEW & PROBLEM

Small retail stores drowning in manual stock tracking

Small and medium retail stores selling traditional garments — abayas, thobes, hijabs — manage inventory through spreadsheets, paper logs, or memory. This creates three critical problems: owners can't see real-time stock levels across sizes and colors, shoppers waste time physically searching racks for their size (often finding it sold out), and restocking decisions are reactive rather than data-driven.

The opportunity: a purpose-built web inventory tool designed specifically for the attributes that matter in garment retail — size, color, material, and quantity — with a clean owner-facing dashboard and a customer-facing availability view.

"I never know what we have in stock until a customer asks and I have to go check in the back. It's embarrassing and slow."

— Store owner · Discovery interview
02 / USER GOALS (JOBS-TO-BE-DONE)

Two very different users, one system

CIMS serves two distinct user types with fundamentally different goals. I mapped both through Jobs-to-be-Done interviews before writing a single feature spec.

UserJob-to-be-DoneCurrent painCIMS solution
Store Owner Clearly see full inventory (sizes, colors, quantities), add/edit/delete items, make restocking decisions confidently Spreadsheets, paper logs, manual counting — slow and error-prone Dashboard with live stock table, inline edit, low-stock alerts, bulk import
Customer / Shopper Know which sizes are in stock without physically searching racks or trying on items that may not be available Wasted time at store, frustration, abandoned purchases Public-facing size availability view — filterable by garment type and color
03 / COMPETITIVE RESEARCH & APPROACH

Existing tools built for the wrong customer

I researched existing inventory management systems — Shopify, Square, Lightspeed, and generic spreadsheet templates. Key finding: all of them are built for general retail or e-commerce at scale. They over-index on POS integration, barcode scanning, and multi-location management. For a single-location traditional garment store, they are overwhelming, expensive, and don't surface the attributes that actually matter (garment type, material, modesty sizing conventions).

This validated the opportunity for a focused, purpose-built tool — clean, structured layout with bold visuals and simple, intuitive navigation designed specifically for small business owners who are not technical power users.

I used Figma to design the system end-to-end, focusing on information density that doesn't feel dense, and a component library that could be handed to a developer with zero ambiguity.

04 / FEATURE PRIORITIZATION

What ships in v1 vs. what waits

With a solo PM/designer setup, scope discipline was critical. I ran a MoSCoW prioritization exercise anchored to the two use cases, then validated priority order with two store owner interviews.

Inventory table — view all items with size/color/qtyMustOwnerCore job #1 — without this, nothing else matters
Add new inventory itemMustOwnerSystem is useless if you can't populate it
Edit existing item attributesMustOwnerInventory changes daily — editing is non-negotiable
Delete / archive itemMustOwnerOutdated stock creates confusion — removal is essential

05 / DESIGN DECISIONS

Clean, structured, built for non-technical users

Inventory table as the home screen. No dashboard widgets, no charts on the first screen. Store owners need to see their stock immediately — the inventory table is the first thing you see on login. Summary stats (total SKUs, low-stock count, out-of-stock count) sit above it as lightweight context, not the focus.

Inline editing, not modal forms. Early wireframes used a modal form for editing items. User testing showed owners preferred clicking directly on a table cell to edit — familiar from spreadsheet muscle memory. We switched to inline editing with a save-on-blur pattern.

Color as a visual attribute, not just a label. In garment retail, color is critical. Rather than just a text field, color swatches render inline in the table — owners can see at a glance that they have 3 units of the navy abaya vs. 1 of the black. This required a custom color picker component but was worth the effort.

Separate customer view URL. The customer-facing size availability page is a distinct, public route — no login required, no pricing shown, just garment name + available sizes with in-stock / low-stock / out-of-stock indicators. Store owners can share this link directly with customers via WhatsApp or Instagram.

06 / REFLECTION & NEXT BETS

What I'd build in v2

Restock purchase order workflow (P0): The logical next step after seeing low-stock alerts is creating a purchase order to the supplier. Right now, owners have to leave the app to do this. A simple PO generator (generate PDF of low-stock items with quantities needed) would close that loop with minimal engineering.

Sales deduction integration (P1): Currently, owners manually update quantities when items sell. If the store uses a POS system, an integration that automatically decrements stock on sale would eliminate the manual update burden entirely — the biggest remaining friction point.

Mobile-optimized owner view (P1): Many store owners manage their store from a phone while on the floor. The current design is responsive but not mobile-first. A dedicated mobile flow for quick quantity adjustments ("just sold 2 of the medium white thobe") would be high-frequency and high-value.

Multi-language support (Could): The target user base for traditional garment retail is predominantly Arabic-speaking. An Arabic/RTL layout was deferred from v1 due to scope, but is an important accessibility and adoption consideration for v2.
